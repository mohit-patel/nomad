In search of dark skies or NGC 2244

“I have every useless thing in the world in my house there. The only thing wanting is the necessary thing, a great patch of open sky like this. Always try to keep a patch of sky above your life, little boy,” he added, turning to me. “You have a soul in you of rare quality, an artist’s nature; never let it starve for lack of what it needs.”
~Proust

So after a long hiatus I relearnt the maddeningly complex process of getting deep sky images. Technically everything went surprisingly well. The horribly light polluted city left me with no result. There was supposed to be red nebulosity in this image. There is absolutely nothing in it. This image is quite a failure. 4 hours of imaging session. 150 images stacked into one.

As consolation I did get a star cluster which I would normally never have observed. This is NGC 2244 (also known as Caldwell 50). It is an open cluster in the Rosette Nebula, which is located in the constellation Monoceros.

The central bright stars are “O stars” so intensely bright and hot that they are planetary danger zones; two of them 400000 times brighter than our sun.

To me technicals are secondary. It’s the process; to be able to see something 5000 light years away, from my home in this decrepit little city and planet using our ingenuity, is magical.
